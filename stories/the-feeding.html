Psychological Horror

The Feeding

25 min read 2024

The first thing Marcus became aware of was the cold. Not the cold of winter or the cold of a room left unheated, but the cold of concrete, of stone, of something that had never known warmth and never would. It seeped through his clothes, through his skin, into his bones, and he knew—even before he opened his eyes—that he was somewhere he shouldn't be.

He opened his eyes to darkness. Not complete darkness—there was a dim light somewhere above, so faint it seemed to be dying even as he looked at it. But it was enough to see the walls. Gray. Concrete. Unadorned. Close enough that he could touch both sides if he stretched out his arms.

He was lying on a cot. A thin mattress, a thinner blanket. He sat up, and the movement sent a wave of nausea through him. His head throbbed. His mouth tasted of something metallic, something wrong.

"Hello?" His voice came out as a croak, barely audible even to himself. "Is anyone there?"

No answer. Only the echo of his own voice, bouncing off the walls and coming back to him, distorted and strange.

He stood, his legs unsteady. The room—the cell, he realized—was small. Ten feet by ten feet, maybe. A toilet in one corner. A sink next to it. And on the wall opposite the cot, a phone. An old phone, the kind with a rotary dial, black and heavy and out of place.

Marcus walked to the phone. He picked up the receiver. Dead. No dial tone, no sound at all. He hung it up and turned, surveying his prison.

There was a door, but no handle on the inside. A slot near the bottom, perhaps six inches tall and a foot wide. For food, he supposed. For whatever they—whoever they were—would give him.

He had no memory of how he got here. No memory of what had happened before. His mind was a blank, a void, and when he tried to reach into it, to find something, anything, he came up empty.

Chapter One: The First Call

Time passed. He had no way to measure it, no clock, no window, no way to tell day from night. He sat on the cot. He stood. He paced. He tried the door. He examined the walls, looking for cracks, for weaknesses, for anything that might offer escape.

There was nothing.

Eventually, the slot in the door opened. A tray slid through, and on it was a bowl. In the bowl was something black, thick, viscous. It looked like tar, like oil, like something that had no business being called food.

Marcus stared at it. His stomach growled. He was hungry, desperately hungry, but the thought of eating that... that sludge made him want to retch.

But hunger won out. He picked up the bowl, brought it to his lips. The smell was indescribable—earthy, metallic, wrong. But he was hungry. So hungry.

He took a sip. It was warm, almost hot, and it tasted like nothing he had ever tasted before. Not good, not bad, just... different. He drank more. And more. Until the bowl was empty.

Almost immediately, he felt better. The hunger was gone. The cold seemed less intense. The fear, the panic, receded. He felt... calm. Peaceful. As if nothing mattered, as if this cell, this prison, was exactly where he was supposed to be.

That was when the phone rang.

The sound was jarring, loud in the silence. Marcus jumped, his heart racing. He stared at the phone. It rang again. And again.

He walked to it, slowly, hesitantly. He picked up the receiver.

"Hello?"

"Marcus? Oh my God, Marcus, is that you?"

The voice was familiar. His sister, Sarah. Relief flooded through him.

"Sarah? Where am I? What's happening?"

"Marcus, you've been gone for three days. Three days! We've been looking everywhere for you. The police... they want to talk to you. They say... they say you killed her, Marcus. They say you killed your wife."

The words hit him like a physical blow. Killed his wife? No. That was impossible. He loved her. He would never...

"What? Sarah, that's crazy. I didn't... I couldn't..."

"They found her body, Marcus. In your house. They found evidence. They're charging you with murder. Where are you? You need to come in, you need to turn yourself in."

"I don't know where I am. I'm in some kind of cell, some kind of prison. I don't remember how I got here. I don't remember..."

"Marcus, listen to me. You need to get out of there. You need to come home. We can figure this out, we can..."

The line went dead. Marcus stood there, the receiver in his hand, listening to the silence. Killed his wife? No. That couldn't be right. He would remember something like that. Wouldn't he?

Chapter Two: The Feeding

Time passed. Marcus had no way to know how much. Hours? Days? The light never changed. The cell never changed. Only he changed, and he wasn't sure if that was for better or worse.

The slot opened again. Another bowl of the black sludge. Marcus took it eagerly this time. He was hungry again, so hungry, and the sludge... the sludge made everything better. It made the fear go away. It made the confusion go away. It made him feel whole, complete, as if this cell was his home, as if he had never known anything else.

He drank it down, every last drop. And as he did, he felt that peace again, that calm, that sense that everything was exactly as it should be.

But this time, something was different. This time, he saw things. Shapes in the darkness, moving just at the edge of his vision. Shadows that weren't quite shadows, forms that weren't quite forms. They moved, they shifted, they watched.

He tried to ignore them. Tried to tell himself they weren't real, that they were just tricks of the light, just his mind playing games. But they were there. He could see them. He could feel them watching.

The phone rang again.

"Marcus? Marcus, are you there?"

Sarah's voice. But something was wrong with it. It sounded... different. Distorted. As if it were coming from far away, or through water, or from somewhere that wasn't quite real.

"Sarah? I'm here. I'm still here."

"Marcus, you need to come home. You need to face what you did. You can't hide forever. The police are looking for you. They're going to find you."

"I didn't do anything, Sarah. I didn't kill her. I couldn't have."

"But you did, Marcus. You did. And you need to remember. You need to remember what you did."

The line went dead. Marcus hung up the phone, his hands shaking. He didn't kill her. He couldn't have. He loved her. He would remember something like that. Wouldn't he?

The shadows in the corner moved. He turned, but they were gone. Or maybe they had never been there. Maybe it was the sludge. Maybe it was making him see things, making him hallucinate.

But he was hungry again. So hungry. And the sludge... the sludge would make it better. It always made it better.

Chapter Three: The Addiction

The feedings came regularly. Marcus couldn't tell how often—time had lost all meaning—but they came, and each time, he took the bowl eagerly. The hunger was overwhelming, all-consuming. It was worse than any hunger he had ever known, worse than anything he could imagine.

But the sludge... the sludge made it go away. It made everything go away. The fear, the confusion, the memories—or lack of memories—the guilt, the shame. All of it, gone. Replaced by peace, by calm, by the certainty that this was where he belonged, that this cell was his home, that he never wanted to leave.

The phone rang. Sarah again.

"Marcus, you need to come home. You can't stay there forever. You need to face what you did."

"I don't want to leave," Marcus said, and he was surprised to realize it was true. He didn't want to leave. This cell, this prison, felt like home. The sludge, the feedings, felt like life. Everything else—the outside world, his sister, his wife, his past—felt like a dream, like something that had never been real.

"Marcus, what's wrong with you? You're not thinking clearly. You need to get out of there. You need to come home."

"I am home," Marcus said. "This is where I belong."

The line went dead. Marcus hung up. He didn't care. He didn't want to talk to Sarah. He didn't want to talk to anyone. He just wanted the sludge. He just wanted the next feeding.

The shadows were back. They were clearer now, more defined. He could see shapes in them, forms. Human forms. Watching him. Waiting.

He didn't care. The sludge would make them go away. The sludge made everything go away.

Chapter Four: The Hallucinations

The hallucinations grew stronger. The shadows became people. People he knew. People from his past. They stood in the corners of the cell, watching him, their eyes empty, their faces blank.

He saw his wife. Standing in the corner, watching him. Her face was pale, her eyes were dark, and there was something wrong with her neck, something that made him look away, that made his stomach turn.

But the sludge made it better. The sludge always made it better.

The phone rang.

"Marcus?"

Sarah's voice. But it didn't sound like Sarah. It sounded like... it sounded like his wife. Like Emma. But that was impossible. Emma was dead. Sarah had said so. The police had said so.

"Marcus, can you hear me? It's me. It's Emma."

No. That wasn't right. That wasn't Sarah. That was...

"Emma? But you're... you're dead. Sarah said you were dead."

"I'm not dead, Marcus. I'm here. I'm always here. I've been here all along. Every time you picked up the phone, it was me. Every time you heard Sarah's voice, it was me. I've been trying to reach you, trying to make you remember."

"Remember what?"

"Remember what you did. Remember what happened. Remember me."

Marcus's hands were shaking. The receiver felt heavy, too heavy. He wanted to hang up, to throw the phone away, to never answer it again. But he couldn't. He had to listen. He had to know.

"I don't remember," he said. "I don't remember anything."

"You will," Emma's voice said. "You will remember. And when you do, you'll wish you hadn't."

The line went dead. Marcus dropped the phone. It clattered to the floor, the sound echoing in the small cell. He backed away from it, his heart racing, his breath coming in gasps.

The shadows were moving. They were coming closer. He could see them now, clearly. People. Dead people. Watching him. Waiting.

He needed the sludge. He needed it now. The hunger was overwhelming, all-consuming. He would do anything for it, anything at all.

Chapter Five: The Memory

The slot opened. The bowl came through. Marcus grabbed it, his hands shaking, and drank it down, every last drop. The peace came, the calm, but it was weaker now. The hallucinations were stronger. The shadows were clearer. The voices were louder.

The phone rang.

Marcus didn't want to answer it. He didn't want to hear Emma's voice, didn't want to hear what she had to say. But he couldn't help himself. He picked up the receiver.

"Marcus?"

Emma's voice. But there was something else. Background noise. Sounds. Voices. A struggle. A fight.

"Marcus, please. Please don't. I love you. I love you so much. Please..."

And then another voice. A voice he recognized. His own voice.

"You don't love me. You never loved me. You were going to leave me. You were going to take everything."

"No, Marcus, that's not true. I would never..."

"Liar. You're a liar. You've always been a liar."

A sound. A terrible sound. A choking sound. A gurgling sound. The sound of something breaking, something tearing, something ending.

And then silence. Long, terrible silence.

"I'm sorry," his own voice said. "I'm so sorry. I didn't mean to... I didn't want to..."

But it was too late. The damage was done. The deed was done. The memory was there, in his mind, clear and terrible and real.

He remembered. He remembered everything.

The fight. The argument. The accusations. The rage. The hands around her neck. The pressure. The breaking. The silence.

He remembered killing her. He remembered watching the life leave her eyes. He remembered the horror, the guilt, the shame. He remembered running, hiding, trying to escape what he had done.

And he remembered this place. This cell. This prison. Not a prison built by others, but a prison built by himself. A prison of guilt, of shame, of self-punishment. A place where he could hide from what he had done, where he could forget, where he could be fed the sludge of denial, of self-deception, of lies.

The phone went dead. Marcus dropped it. He looked around the cell, at the walls, at the cot, at the toilet, at the sink. And at the phone.

Except there was no phone.

He stared at the wall where the phone had been. There was nothing there. No phone. No receiver. No cord. Nothing. Just a blank wall, gray and concrete and empty.

But he had answered it. He had talked to Sarah. He had talked to Emma. He had heard his own voice, heard himself killing her. He had held the receiver in his hand, felt its weight, heard the dial tone, heard the voices.

But there was no phone.

The slot opened. Another bowl of sludge. Marcus stared at it. He was hungry, so hungry. The hunger was overwhelming, all-consuming. He needed it. He needed the sludge, needed the peace, needed the forgetting.

But he also needed to remember. He needed to face what he had done. He needed to accept it, to own it, to be punished for it.

He picked up the bowl. He looked at the black sludge, at the thing that had kept him here, that had kept him from remembering, that had kept him from facing the truth.

And he threw it against the wall.

The sludge splattered, black and thick, running down the concrete. The hunger came back, stronger than ever, overwhelming, all-consuming. But he resisted. He had to resist. He had to remember. He had to face what he had done.

The shadows moved. They came closer. He could see them now, clearly. The people he had hurt, the people he had killed, the people he had destroyed. They were all there, watching him, waiting.

And in the corner, he saw her. Emma. His wife. The woman he had killed. She stood there, watching him, her eyes empty, her face pale, and there was something wrong with her neck, something that made him look away, that made his stomach turn.

"I'm sorry," he said. "I'm so sorry. I didn't mean to... I didn't want to..."

But it was too late. The damage was done. The deed was done. The memory was there, in his mind, clear and terrible and real.

The slot opened again. Another bowl. Another feeding. Another chance to forget, to escape, to hide.

Marcus stared at it. The hunger was overwhelming. He needed it. He needed the sludge, needed the peace, needed the forgetting.

But he also needed to remember. He needed to face what he had done. He needed to accept it, to own it, to be punished for it.

He picked up the bowl. He looked at the black sludge. And he drank it down, every last drop.

The peace came. The calm. The forgetting. But it was weaker now. The memory was stronger. The truth was stronger. The guilt, the shame, the horror—they were all stronger.

He was in a cell. A prison. A place of punishment. But it wasn't a prison built by others. It was a prison built by himself. A prison of guilt, of shame, of self-punishment. A place where he could hide from what he had done, where he could forget, where he could be fed the sludge of denial, of self-deception, of lies.

But the lies were breaking down. The denial was failing. The self-deception was crumbling. The truth was coming through, clear and terrible and real.

He had killed her. He had killed his wife. He had taken her life, had destroyed her, had ended her. And now he was here, in this cell, in this prison, being fed the sludge of his own guilt, his own shame, his own self-punishment.

The phone rang.

But there was no phone.

Marcus looked at the wall where the phone had been. There was nothing there. No phone. No receiver. No cord. Nothing. Just a blank wall, gray and concrete and empty.

But the ringing continued. And he knew, with a certainty that chilled him to the bone, that it would never stop. That the voices would never stop. That the memory would never stop. That the guilt, the shame, the horror, would never stop.

He was in a cell. A prison. A place of punishment. And he would never leave. He would never escape. He would never forget.

The feeding would continue. The sludge would continue. The forgetting would continue. But it would never be enough. The memory would always come back. The truth would always come through. The guilt, the shame, the horror—they would always be there, waiting, watching, reminding him of what he had done.

He had killed her. He had killed his wife. And now he was here, in this cell, in this prison, being fed the sludge of his own guilt, his own shame, his own self-punishment.

The phone rang.

But there was no phone.

In the silence of the cell, in the darkness of the prison, the feeding continues. And the memory remains. Always. Forever.
