The Last Transmission

15 min read 2024

The message arrived at 03:47 ship time, breaking the monotony of deep space silence that had persisted for the last six months. Dr. Elena Vasquez was reviewing spectral data from the nebula when the alert chimed—a sound she hadn't heard since leaving Earth's solar system.

"Captain, we have an incoming transmission," she said into her comm, her voice steady despite the tremor in her hands. "Origin unknown. Frequency... it's using our emergency band, but the encryption doesn't match any known protocol."

Captain Marcus Chen's voice crackled through the speakers. "Unknown origin? Elena, we're three light-years from the nearest outpost. Who could be transmitting?"

That was the question, wasn't it? The Aether was the first vessel to venture this far into uncharted space. They were alone. Or so they thought.

Elena decoded the message, her fingers flying across the console. The text appeared on her screen, stark and simple:

"Turn back. They are waiting. Coordinates: 47.3, -12.8, 9.1. Do not approach. Do not investigate. Turn back."

The coordinates were embedded in the message, pointing to a location just two days' travel from their current position. But according to all their charts, that location was empty space. Nothing. A void.

Chapter One: The Anomaly

Captain Chen assembled the crew in the observation deck. The message played on the main screen, its warning echoing in the silence that followed.

"Thoughts?" Chen asked, his eyes scanning the faces of his team.

Dr. James Morrison, the ship's astrophysicist, was the first to speak. "The message is in English. Perfect English. No translation errors, no linguistic drift. That's statistically impossible unless..."

"Unless what?" Elena prompted.

"Unless it's from us. From Earth. Or from someone who's been studying us for a very long time."

The implications hung in the air like smoke. If the message was from Earth, it would have taken years to reach them. But the timestamp embedded in the transmission showed it was sent only hours ago. That was impossible. Unless time itself was behaving differently out here in the void.

"We have a mission," Chen said finally. "We're here to explore, to discover. This message—this anomaly—is exactly what we came to find."

Elena felt a chill that had nothing to do with the ship's climate control. "Captain, the message explicitly warns us not to investigate. Shouldn't we at least consider—"

"Consider what? Turning back because of an anonymous warning? We're scientists, Elena. We don't run from mysteries. We solve them."

Chapter Two: The Planet That Shouldn't Exist

Two days later, they arrived at the coordinates. And there it was.

A planet. A world that appeared on their sensors as if it had always been there, yet their star charts showed nothing. It was a dark, rocky sphere, roughly the size of Mars, orbiting a star that didn't appear on any astronomical database.

"This is impossible," Morrison whispered, staring at the readings. "The star... it's not on any chart. Not in any catalog. It's as if it appeared overnight."

Elena ran the calculations three times. Each time, the result was the same. According to all known physics, this star system shouldn't exist. The gravitational signatures were wrong. The light spectra were wrong. Everything was wrong.

"Captain," she said, "I'm detecting artificial structures on the planet's surface. Massive ones. They're... they're arranged in patterns. Mathematical patterns."

The structures appeared on the screen—geometric shapes that defied explanation. Pyramids, but not quite. Spheres, but with impossible angles. They seemed to shift when you looked at them, as if they existed in more than three dimensions.

"We need to get closer," Chen said. "Prepare a landing party."

"Captain, I strongly advise against this," Elena said. "The message was clear. We don't know what's down there. We don't know who sent that warning, or why."

"That's exactly why we need to find out," Chen replied. "Elena, you're with me. Morrison, you too. We're going down."

Chapter Three: First Contact

The surface of the planet was unlike anything Elena had ever seen. The ground was a deep purple-black, and it seemed to absorb light rather than reflect it. The air was breathable, but it tasted wrong—metallic, with an undertone of something she couldn't identify.

They approached the nearest structure, a massive pyramid that seemed to shift and shimmer in their vision. As they drew closer, Elena realized the structure wasn't made of stone or metal. It was made of something else entirely—something that seemed to be both solid and not solid at the same time.

"Do you hear that?" Morrison asked, his voice barely above a whisper.

Elena listened. At first, she heard nothing. Then, gradually, a sound began to emerge. It was like music, but not music. Like speech, but not speech. It was a pattern, a rhythm that seemed to resonate in her bones.

"It's coming from the structure," Chen said, his hand moving toward his weapon. "Stay alert."

They reached the base of the pyramid. Up close, it was even more disorienting. The surface seemed to be made of shifting geometric patterns that hurt to look at directly. Elena felt a headache building behind her eyes.

Then, without warning, the surface of the pyramid opened. Not a door, not an entrance— the material simply... parted, revealing a passageway that led into darkness.

"Captain," Elena said, "we should turn back. Now."

But Chen was already moving forward, his light illuminating the passage. "This is what we came for, Elena. First contact. This is history."

They entered the structure. The passageway was narrow, the walls covered in symbols that seemed to writhe and shift. Elena's translator couldn't make sense of them— they weren't language as humans understood it. They were something else.

The passage opened into a vast chamber. In the center, suspended in mid-air, was a sphere of pure darkness. Not black—darkness. A void that seemed to absorb all light, all sound, all thought.

"What is that?" Morrison asked.

As if in response, the sphere began to change. Images appeared on its surface— images of Earth, of the Aether, of the crew. But the images were wrong. They showed things that hadn't happened yet. They showed the ship being destroyed. They showed the crew dying. They showed Earth being consumed by darkness.

"It's showing us the future," Elena realized. "Or... a possible future."

The images shifted again. Now they showed the message being sent. They showed Elena herself, years older, sending the transmission. They showed her warning her past self not to come here.

"No," Elena whispered. "That's not possible."

But the sphere showed more. It showed what would happen if they didn't turn back. It showed the Aether being pulled into the planet's orbit, unable to escape. It showed the crew being changed, transformed into something that was no longer human. It showed them becoming part of the planet, part of the structures, part of whatever intelligence had built this place.

Chapter Four: The Choice

"We need to leave," Elena said, her voice urgent. "Now. Before it's too late."

"Elena, we can't just—" Chen began, but she cut him off.

"Captain, look at the images. That's us. That's our future if we stay. The message was from me. From future me. I'm warning us to turn back because I know what happens if we don't."

The sphere showed one final image: the Aether, transformed, returning to Earth. Bringing the darkness with it. Spreading it across the planet, across the solar system, across the galaxy.

"This isn't a planet," Elena realized. "It's a trap. A prison. And we're about to become its next inhabitants."

They ran. Back through the passageway, back to their shuttle, back to the Aether. Behind them, the structures began to move, to shift, to reach for them. But they made it. They escaped.

As the Aether pulled away from the planet, Elena looked back one last time. The structures were still moving, still shifting. And she could swear she saw other ships—ships from other times, other realities—trapped in orbit around the dark world.

"We're not going back to Earth," Chen said, his voice heavy. "Not yet. We need to warn others. We need to make sure no one else finds this place."

Elena nodded. She understood now. The message she would send—the message she had already sent—wasn't just a warning. It was a loop. A temporal loop that would keep repeating, keep trying to prevent the future she had seen.

But as the Aether moved further away, she couldn't shake the feeling that they were already too late. That the darkness had already touched them. That they were already changed, already part of something larger and more terrible than they could imagine.

In the void between stars, the planet waited. And it would keep waiting, for the next ship, the next crew, the next victims. Because that was its purpose. That was what it did.

Turn back. They are waiting.
