The Wilderness

22 min read 2024

The parking lot was empty when Marcus arrived at Wilderness Battlefield Park, except for one other car—an old, rusted sedan that looked like it had been abandoned years ago. The sun was setting, painting the sky in shades of orange and purple that should have been beautiful but instead looked like a wound, like blood spreading across the horizon.

Marcus got out of his car, his hiking boots crunching on the gravel. He'd been planning this hike for weeks, looking forward to the solitude, the peace, the chance to clear his head. But now, standing here in the fading light, he felt something else. Unease. A sense that he shouldn't be here, that this wasn't the right time, that something was wrong.

He tried to shake it off. It was just nerves, just the natural apprehension that came with being alone in a place with a dark history. The Wilderness had been the site of one of the bloodiest battles of the Civil War. Thousands had died here. Thousands more had gone missing, their bodies never found, their fates unknown. It was natural to feel uneasy. It was natural to feel watched.

But the feeling persisted. And as he walked toward the trailhead, he saw someone. A man, standing near the information kiosk, his back to Marcus, his form silhouetted against the setting sun. There was something wrong with him. Something off. His posture was wrong, his stance was wrong, the way he held himself was wrong. Like he wasn't quite human, like he was something else, something that was trying to look human but wasn't quite getting it right.

Marcus slowed, his heart rate picking up, his palms getting sweaty. The man turned, and Marcus saw his face. Or what should have been his face. The features were there—eyes, nose, mouth—but they were wrong. Too sharp, too angular, too predatory. And the eyes... the eyes were empty. Not blank, not dead, but empty. Like there was nothing behind them, like they were just holes, just voids, just nothing.

The man smiled, and the smile was wrong too. Too wide, too sharp, too hungry. Like it wasn't a smile at all, but a threat, a warning, a promise of something terrible.

"Evening," the man said, his voice wrong, too smooth, too practiced, like he was reciting lines he'd memorized but didn't understand. "Beautiful night for a hike."

Marcus nodded, not trusting his voice, not wanting to engage, not wanting to stay. "Yeah. Beautiful."

"Be careful out there," the man said, his smile widening, his eyes staying empty. "The woods can be dangerous. Especially at dusk. Especially here. Especially now."

Marcus felt a chill run down his spine. The words were a warning, but they didn't sound like a warning. They sounded like a promise. Like a threat. Like something that was going to happen, that was already happening, that couldn't be stopped.

"I'll be careful," Marcus said, backing away, wanting to leave, wanting to get in his car and drive away and never come back. But something stopped him. Pride, maybe. Stubbornness. The unwillingness to be scared off by a creepy stranger, to let fear control him, to run away from something that was probably nothing, that was probably just his imagination, that was probably just the natural unease that came with being alone in a place with a dark history.

He walked past the man, toward the trailhead, toward the sign that marked the beginning of the path. And as he passed, he felt the man's eyes on him, watching, waiting, hungry. But he didn't look back. He kept walking, kept moving, kept telling himself it was nothing, that he was being paranoid, that everything was fine.

Chapter One: The Sign

The sign at the trailhead was old, weathered, the wood warped and cracked, the paint faded and peeling. But the words were still legible, still clear, still stark:

"WARNING: Multiple hikers have gone missing in this area. If you become disoriented, stay where you are and call for help. Do not venture off the marked trails. The Wilderness can be dangerous, especially at dusk and after dark."

Below the warning was a list. Names. Dates. People who had gone missing, who had never been found, who had disappeared into the woods and never come back. There were dozens of them, stretching back years, decades, all the way back to the Civil War, when soldiers had vanished in the confusion of battle, their bodies never recovered, their fates unknown.

Marcus read the names, his stomach tightening, his unease growing. So many. So many people who had come here, who had walked these trails, who had disappeared. Never found. Never recovered. Just gone, vanished, lost to the Wilderness.

He should turn back. He should leave. He should get in his car and drive away and never come back. But he didn't. He kept reading, kept looking, kept telling himself it was just a warning, just a precaution, just something to keep people safe, to keep them on the trails, to keep them from getting lost.

But the names. The dates. The sheer number of them. It didn't feel like a warning. It felt like a list. A catalog. A record of people who had come here and never left, who had been taken, who had been claimed by something that lived in the woods, that waited in the darkness, that hungered.

Marcus shook his head, trying to clear it, trying to shake off the unease, the fear, the sense that something was wrong. He was being paranoid. He was letting his imagination run away with him. He was reading too much into a warning sign, into a list of missing people, into a creepy stranger who was probably just a local, probably just weird, probably just harmless.

He took a deep breath, shouldered his backpack, and started down the trail. The sun was almost gone now, the light fading fast, the shadows lengthening, the darkness gathering. But he kept walking. He'd come here to hike, to clear his head, to find some peace. And he wasn't going to let fear stop him. He wasn't going to let a warning sign, or a list of missing people, or a creepy stranger, keep him from what he'd come here to do.

But as he walked, as the light faded, as the darkness grew, he couldn't shake the feeling that he was being watched. That something was following him. That something was waiting. And that he was walking into something he couldn't escape, something he couldn't fight, something that had been waiting for him, that had been calling to him, that had been drawing him here, to this place, to this moment, to this end.

Chapter Two: The Trail

The trail wound through dense woods, the trees pressing close on either side, their branches reaching out like skeletal fingers, like claws, like something that wanted to grab him, to hold him, to keep him. The path was narrow, barely wide enough for one person, and it was getting darker, the shadows deepening, the light fading, the world around him becoming a place of shapes and sounds and things he couldn't quite see, couldn't quite identify, couldn't quite understand.

Marcus walked slowly, carefully, his flashlight in his hand but not turned on yet. He wanted to preserve his night vision, wanted to see as much as he could in the fading light, wanted to be aware of his surroundings, of what was around him, of what might be following him.

Because something was following him. He was sure of it now. He could hear it—footsteps, soft, careful, trying to be quiet but not quite succeeding. He could feel it—a presence, a weight, a sense of being watched, of being stalked, of being hunted. He could smell it—something wrong, something that didn't belong, something that was decay and rot and death.

He stopped, turning around, his heart pounding, his breath coming in short gasps. But there was nothing there. Just the trail, just the trees, just the darkness. Nothing following him. Nothing watching him. Nothing hunting him.

But he knew. He knew something was there. He knew something was following him. He knew something was waiting. And he knew he had to keep moving, had to keep walking, had to get to the end of the trail, to safety, to his car, to escape.

He started walking again, faster now, his pace quickening, his heart racing, his fear growing. And as he walked, he began to notice things. Things that shouldn't be there. Things that didn't make sense. Things that were wrong.

The trees. They looked different. Older. More gnarled. More twisted. Like they'd been here for centuries, like they'd seen things, like they'd witnessed things, like they'd been part of something terrible, something that had left its mark on them, that had changed them, that had made them something else.

The ground. It felt different. Softer. More yielding. Like it was made of something else, something that wasn't earth, something that was decay, something that was death. And when he looked down, when he really looked, he could see things. Bones. Fragments of bone, pieces of what had once been people, scattered in the dirt, half-buried, forgotten, lost.

The air. It smelled different. Wrong. Like gunpowder. Like smoke. Like blood. Like death. Like something that had happened here, that was still happening here, that would always happen here.

And then he heard it. A sound. Distant, at first, but getting closer. The sound of musket fire. The sound of battle. The sound of war. And with it, voices. Shouts. Screams. The sounds of men fighting, of men dying, of men being torn apart, of men being lost, of men being claimed by something that lived in the woods, that waited in the darkness, that hungered.

Chapter Three: The Past

Marcus stopped again, his whole body trembling, his mind reeling, his world tilting. The sounds were getting louder, closer, more real. And with them came images. Shapes in the darkness. Figures moving through the trees. Men in uniforms, blue and gray, fighting, dying, being torn apart.

He saw them. Civil War soldiers. Ghosts. Spirits. Whatever they were, they were there, they were real, they were happening. He could see their faces, their expressions, their fear, their pain, their death. He could hear their voices, their shouts, their screams, their last words, their final moments.

And he could feel it. The battle. The war. The death. The loss. The thousands of men who had died here, who had been lost here, who had been claimed by something that lived in the woods, that waited in the darkness, that hungered.

He started running. Not thinking, not planning, just running, just trying to escape, to get away, to leave this place, to never come back. But the trail seemed to go on forever, seemed to stretch into infinity, seemed to lead nowhere, seemed to be a trap, a maze, a prison from which there was no escape.

And the sounds followed him. The musket fire. The battle. The war. The death. The loss. And with them came the images, the ghosts, the spirits, the soldiers who had died here, who had been lost here, who had been claimed by something that lived in the woods, that waited in the darkness, that hungered.

He ran faster, his lungs burning, his legs aching, his whole body screaming in protest. But he couldn't stop. He couldn't slow down. He couldn't rest. Because something was following him. Something was hunting him. Something was waiting for him to stop, to slow down, to rest, so it could catch him, could claim him, could make him one of the missing, one of the lost, one of the claimed.

He looked back, and he saw it. Not clearly, not fully, but enough. A shape. A presence. Something that was following him, that was hunting him, that was waiting. It was dark, shadowy, formless, but it had eyes. Eyes that glowed, that burned, that hungered. And it had a presence, a weight, a sense of wrongness, of evil, of something that shouldn't exist, that shouldn't be, that was an abomination, a violation, a thing that had no right to be, that had no place in the world, that was a mistake, a flaw, a corruption.

A demon. That's what it was. A demon. Something from another place, another time, another reality. Something that had been drawn here by the death, by the loss, by the suffering. Something that had been feeding on it, growing stronger, becoming more real, more solid, more able to cross over, to exist in this world, to claim more victims, to create more death, more loss, more suffering.

And it was following him. It was hunting him. It was waiting for him to stop, to slow down, to rest, so it could catch him, could claim him, could make him one of the missing, one of the lost, one of the claimed.

Chapter Four: The Chase

Marcus ran, his whole body screaming, his mind reeling, his world collapsing. The trail twisted and turned, leading him deeper into the woods, deeper into the darkness, deeper into the past, deeper into the place where the battle had been fought, where the soldiers had died, where the demon had been born, where it had been feeding, where it had been growing, where it had been waiting.

The sounds grew louder. The musket fire. The battle. The war. The death. The loss. And with them came the images, clearer now, more real, more present. He could see the soldiers, their faces, their expressions, their fear, their pain, their death. He could see the battle, the chaos, the confusion, the horror. He could see the death, the loss, the suffering, the thousands of men who had died here, who had been lost here, who had been claimed by something that lived in the woods, that waited in the darkness, that hungered.

And he could feel it. The demon. Getting closer. Getting stronger. Getting more real. Its presence was a weight, a pressure, a sense of wrongness that made his skin crawl, that made his stomach turn, that made him want to vomit, to scream, to run, to hide, to disappear, to never exist, to never have been born.

He looked back again, and this time he saw it clearly. The demon. It was huge, towering, a thing of darkness and shadow and wrongness. It had no form, no shape, no body, but it had presence, it had weight, it had reality. And it had eyes. Eyes that glowed, that burned, that hungered. Eyes that saw him, that knew him, that wanted him, that needed him, that would have him, that would claim him, that would make him one of the missing, one of the lost, one of the claimed.

Marcus ran faster, his whole body on fire, his mind breaking, his world ending. But it wasn't enough. The demon was faster. The demon was stronger. The demon was closer. And he knew, with a certainty that chilled him to the bone, that he couldn't escape, that he couldn't fight, that he couldn't win. That he was going to die here, in this place, in this darkness, in this past, claimed by something that lived in the woods, that waited in the darkness, that hungered.

He tripped, falling to the ground, his body hitting the dirt with a force that knocked the air from his lungs, that made his head spin, that made his vision blur. He tried to get up, to keep running, to keep fighting, but he couldn't. His body wouldn't respond. His legs wouldn't work. His strength was gone. He was done. He was finished. He was claimed.

He rolled over, looking up, and he saw it. The demon. Standing over him, towering, dark, wrong. Its eyes burned, its presence crushed, its hunger consumed. And it reached for him, its form shifting, changing, becoming something else, something familiar, something he recognized.

The creepy man. The man from the parking lot. The man with the wrong features, the wrong eyes, the wrong smile. The demon had been him all along. The demon had been waiting for him, had been watching for him, had been calling to him, drawing him here, to this place, to this moment, to this end.

"I told you to be careful," the demon said, its voice wrong, too smooth, too practiced, like it was reciting lines it had memorized but didn't understand. "I told you the woods could be dangerous. Especially at dusk. Especially here. Especially now."

Marcus tried to speak, tried to scream, tried to fight, but he couldn't. His voice was gone. His strength was gone. His will was gone. He was done. He was finished. He was claimed.

The demon reached for him, its form shifting, changing, becoming something else, something terrible, something that shouldn't exist, that shouldn't be, that was an abomination, a violation, a thing that had no right to be, that had no place in the world, that was a mistake, a flaw, a corruption.

And then it touched him. And everything changed. The world shifted. Reality fractured. Time collapsed. And Marcus was no longer Marcus. He was something else. Something that had been claimed, that had been taken, that had been made part of the demon, part of the woods, part of the past, part of the battle, part of the death, part of the loss, part of the suffering.

He was one of the missing. He was one of the lost. He was one of the claimed. And he would always be. Forever. Part of the demon. Part of the woods. Part of the past. Part of the battle. Part of the death. Part of the loss. Part of the suffering.

The demon smiled, its expression wrong, too wide, too sharp, too hungry. And then it was gone, vanished, disappeared into the darkness, into the woods, into the past, waiting for the next victim, the next person who would come here, who would walk these trails, who would be claimed, who would be made part of the missing, part of the lost, part of the claimed.

And Marcus was alone. Alone in the darkness. Alone in the woods. Alone in the past. Alone with the battle, with the death, with the loss, with the suffering. Alone with the demon, with the thing that had claimed him, that had made him part of itself, that had made him one of the missing, one of the lost, one of the claimed.

Forever.

In the Wilderness, the past never dies. The battle never ends. The soldiers never rest. And the demon never stops. It waits. It watches. It hungers. And it claims. Forever.
